How to Measure Your Monthly Returns on New Investments as a Beginner

Learn how to effectively evaluate your monthly investment returns with concrete strategies designed for US and EU beginners.

Productivity • Updated: 2026-03-11 09:16:53

TL;DR

Log your starting and ending balances each month and include new deposits, fees, and dividends. Use simple ETFs like VTI or IWDA for clarity. Track every month—the magic is in the monthly cadence, not daily noise. Google Sheets or Sharesight are the top beginner tools. Skip complex apps and stick to low-fee, diversified funds for now.

Quick Answer: The Best Way for Beginners to Measure Monthly Returns

Start with a hands-on approach by tracking your monthly returns using either a Google Sheet or a beginner-friendly tool like Sharesight. For a $1,000 investment in a diversified ETF such as Vanguard Total Stock Market (VTI), log the starting value, each deposit, and your balance every month.

Subtract fees, note any dividends, and calculate your net return. This is for anyone just starting and wanting to avoid expensive mistakes while actually learning what their money is doing, step by step.

Avoid complicated trading tools—your first goal is clarity, not complexity.

What Most Beginners Get Wrong About Monthly Returns

The classic mistake: looking only at headline gains and ignoring the drag of fees and timing. Many new investors in both the US and EU misjudge their returns by simply comparing this month’s portfolio value to last month’s, forgetting to factor in new deposits or withdrawals.

Another common trap is ignoring management fees or platform costs—these can shave off real money from your gains and accumulate over time. Time matters, too: checking daily or even weekly can distort your sense of true performance, especially with volatile assets.

Many apps show percentage returns without explaining how deposits and payouts affect the math. For example, reinvesting dividends can misleadingly look like a loss if not tracked properly. Beginners often confuse price changes with real, realized performance. The result? You think you’re beating the market, but after fees, taxes, and bad timing, your net return may actually lag behind a basic index fund.

If you want to see real productivity with your investments, avoid these pitfalls and focus on a monthly cycle. More on this in our checklist of beginner mistakes and in our article on ETF vs. stock performance for first-time investors.

A 30-Day Workflow: How to Evaluate Monthly Returns Step-by-Step

To make monthly returns part of your investing routine, use this repeatable 30-day cadence: 1. On the first of each month, log your starting portfolio value (including cash, stocks, ETFs, and any new deposits). 2. Throughout the month, record every cash input or withdrawal (note the date and amount). 3. At the end of the month, log your portfolio's closing value. Subtract any fees shown in your account (transaction, platform, or management fees). 4. Use this formula for your monthly return percentage: (Ending Value - Starting Value - Deposits + Withdrawals) ÷ (Starting Value + 0.5 × Deposits) × 100%. 5. Note any dividends or interest earned separately—track these as they materially impact total return, especially when reinvested. 6. Compare to a basic benchmark, like the S&P 500 or MSCI World ETF, to see if you’re trending ahead or behind.

Set a recurring reminder so you don’t skip the cycle. This process not only gives you clarity on your actual gains, but helps you identify mistakes or subpar fees quickly. For those wanting a more automated workflow, Sharesight, Personal Capital (US), or Portfolio Performance (EU) are solid beginner tools—but hands-on tracking builds understanding fast.

A Real Scenario: Month-by-Month on a $1,000 ETF Investment

Imagine you start on January 1 with $1,000 in iShares Core MSCI World ETF (IWDA). Let’s walk through three months:

January: You invest $1,000. By month’s end, the value is $1,010. There’s a $1 management fee and $3 brokerage fee. No dividends yet. - Net monthly return: ($1,010 - $1,000 - $0 - fees $4) ÷ $1,000 × 100% = 0.6%.

February: You add $200. End balance is $1,150. Fees total $2. $7 in dividends received and reinvested. - Net monthly return: ($1,150 - $1,010 - $200 + $0 - $2 fees) ÷ ($1,010 + 0.5×$200) × 100% ≈ -5.5% (reflects the timing of deposits, not just price change).

March: No new cash. End balance rises to $1,230. Total fees are $1. Dividend of $5 reinvested. - Net: ($1,230 - $1,150) ÷ $1,150 × 100% ≈ 7%.

You learn quickly that new deposits distort the math, and that skipping fee and dividend adjustments leads to misinformed decisions. Stepwise tracking reveals when your strategy actually beats idle cash or not. For deeper allocations, check our guide to splitting new money across ETFs vs. single stocks.

Mistakes That Cost Real Money: Tracking Returns as a Beginner

Here are two rookie mistakes guaranteed to skew your numbers: 1. Neglecting Reinvestment: If you forget to log reinvested dividends or interest, your real return looks lower (or negative in a down month). Always record those, even if it’s a small $3 payout. 2. Using Outdated Data: Many beginners pull numbers from an app without considering time lag—Friday night’s close isn’t Sunday morning’s; monthly statements usually lag by a day or two, and Google Finance can misprice ETFs, especially outside the US trading window. Lagged or partial data makes it impossible to compare apples-to-apples each month. Validation matters.

Others to watch: overreacting to short-term dips (selling too soon), ignoring cumulative fees in complex portfolios (especially if using robo-advisors), or not tracking taxable events. Each slip reduces the clarity of your monthly snapshot—and can add up to thousands missed by year-end. Which Tools, ETFs, or Resources Should Beginners Use—and Avoid—for Monthly Evaluation?

For beginners, simple often beats flashy dashboards. Start with Google Sheets for manual entry, or opt for Sharesight (global), Portfolio Performance (EU), or Personal Capital (US) for more automated tracking.

Avoid day trading platforms (like Robinhood or Plus500) for now—they encourage rapid-fire trades, not disciplined monthly review. For ETFs, stick with low-fee, broad-market funds: VTI (US), IWDA (EU/UK), or Vanguard FTSE All-World (VWCE) are first picks. These make it easier to compare performance and limit surprise fee hits.

Prioritize tools that allow you to: - Track performance by deposit and withdrawal timing - Add fee and dividend records - Set benchmarks for comparison

Ignore high-frequency trading features, margin tools, or anything that pushes you toward real-time reactions. If you want a more advanced or automated review, read our tool-by-tool comparison and checklist for portfolio monitoring apps.

FAQ

How often should I evaluate my investment returns?
Monthly evaluation is ideal for beginners. It captures trends without being distracted by short-term volatility, and aligns with when most brokers and platforms issue statements. Weekly tracking may help if you’re actively learning about price swings, but skip daily checks—they usually generate stress and not insight.

What tools can help me track my monthly investment performance?
Google Sheets, Sharesight, Personal Capital (for US), and Portfolio Performance (for EU) are beginner-friendly and reliable. They let you record deposits, fees, and performance over time. Avoid tools that focus mainly on day trading or promote excessive notifications.

How do I handle new deposits or withdrawals in my return calculation?
Always adjust your return formula for any new cash moving into or out of your account during the month. Use the modified Dietz formula or similar: monthly net return = (Ending Balance - Starting Balance - Net Deposits) divided by (Starting Balance + 0.5 × Net Deposits). Don’t just look at simple gain or loss.

Are ETF management fees included in monthly returns?
Most ETFs deduct their management fees directly from the fund’s value, so they are reflected in your returns automatically. However, manual tracking should add transaction or platform fees outside the fund fee if your broker charges them.

Should I compare my performance to the S&P 500 or a different benchmark?
For US stocks and ETFs, the S&P 500 is a practical benchmark. If your investment is global (like IWDA or VWCE), use MSCI World or FTSE All-World. Always choose a benchmark that matches your portfolio’s market exposure and currency for a fair comparison.
