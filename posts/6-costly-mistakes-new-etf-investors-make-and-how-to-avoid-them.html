6 Costly Mistakes New ETF Investors Make and How to Avoid Them

Avoid these common ETF investment mistakes that can hurt your returns. Learn strategies to invest smarter now.

Investing • Updated: 2026-03-13 07:21:13

TL;DR

Avoid pitfalls like focusing solely on past performance. Assess fees thoroughly and prioritize diversification to build a resilient ETF investment strategy.

The Hidden Risks: What New ETF Investors Overlook

### The Hidden Risks: What New ETF Investors Overlook

Many new investors underestimate the risks associated with ETFs, often viewing them as a simple pathway to diversification. A common misconception is that a low expense ratio automatically signals a better investment.

For example, while the Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) boasts a low expense ratio of 0.1%, this can be misleading. If the broader market experiences a downturn, even this seemingly conservative ETF can suffer significant losses.

During the COVID-19 market crash in March 2020, for instance, VOO lost nearly 34% of its value in just over a month.

Moreover, the allure of past performance can cloud judgment. Investors often forget that while an ETF may have performed stellar in the past decade—such as the Invesco QQQ, which gained over 500% from 2010 to 2020—this doesn’t guarantee future success. In fact, after hitting a peak in February 2021, QQQ saw a dramatic decline of over 20% by March 2022, illustrating just how quickly favorable sentiment can shift.

New investors also commonly overlook the risks tied to liquidity. Less popular ETFs can experience wider bid-ask spreads—meaning the cost to enter and exit a position can be much higher than expected, especially during periods of market volatility.

For example, an ETF with only a few million in average daily volume may face a bid-ask spread of 1.5% compared to a more liquid ETF with a spread of 0.1%. This difference can eat into an investor's returns significantly, especially for smaller positions.

A case in point involves the Global X Cannabis ETF (POTX), which was launched to capitalize on the burgeoning cannabis industry. While it attracted early interest thanks to a vibrant sector, it quickly became clear that price movements in cannabis stocks could be highly volatile.

Within a year of its debut, POTX had seen intraday fluctuations of over 7%, causing anxiety for investors who may have anticipated a steadily increasing trend. Therefore, beyond just product selection, understanding the inherent market risks and the nature of the underlying assets is vital for new ETF investors.

Recognizing these hidden risks is essential for crafting a robust investment strategy that withstands market turbulence.

Why Relying Solely on Past Performance is a Mistake

Why Relying Solely on Past Performance is a Mistake

Many newcomers make the mistake of selecting ETFs based solely on historical performance data, ignoring fundamental metrics that can signal future viability. For instance, an ETF might demonstrate a remarkable gain of 50% over the past year, enticing you into a hasty investment decision.

However, if this growth stems from temporary market conditions—like a short-lived tech boom—then investing without assessing the underlying companies could be disastrous. A prime example is the Invesco QQQ ETF (QQQ), which surged about 90% from March 2020 to February 2021 during the pandemic as tech stocks flourished.

Yet, as the market corrected in March 2021, QQQ fell by nearly 20% in just a few months, demonstrating that short-term success can quickly turn into losses.

Additionally, many investors fail to understand important sectors and trends that can influence the future performance of ETFs. Take the Financial Select Sector SPDR Fund (XLF), which was particularly appealing in 2021 as interest rates were predicted to rise.

However, as economic uncertainty loomed and a possible recession became more apparent in early 2022, XLF saw a significant downturn, dropping approximately 25% by mid-year. This shift underscores how an ETF that seems viable based on past performance can withstand unanticipated economic pressures.

Another common pitfall is neglecting diversification. For instance, an investor may choose the SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY) due to its historical capitalization, gaining over 60% between 2020 and 2021.

However, a one-fund strategy focused solely on large-cap U.S. equities can leave you exposed to market fluctuations that disproportionately impact that segment. When the market took a nosedive in 2022, SPY fell about 18%, which could have been mitigated through a diversified portfolio that includes international or bond ETFs.

Consider a hypothetical scenario where an investor, Alex, only weighs the recent past performance of an ETF like the ARK Innovation ETF (ARKK), which surged approximately 150% in 2020.

Enticed by these gains, Alex invests heavily, neglecting to investigate the fund's focus on disruptive companies that may face regulatory scrutiny and market saturation. By late 2021, ARKK plummeted nearly 40% as speculative stocks faced extensive sell-offs, leaving Alex with a substantial loss.

The key takeaway here is that while historical performance can provide valuable insights, it should never be the sole basis for investment decisions. By incorporating current market conditions, sector analysis, and understanding the underlying economics driving your chosen ETFs, you can better position yourself to avoid potential pitfalls and enhance your long-term investment success.

The Right Way to Gauge Fees: Hidden Costs of ETFs

### The Right Way to Gauge Fees: Hidden Costs of ETFs

Understanding the complete fee structure of an ETF is vital for new investors, as hidden costs can erode returns significantly. While an expense ratio is often highlighted, trading costs can add up, especially for frequent traders.

For instance, the expense ratio for the iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) is only 0.03%, making it seem like an attractive choice. However, if you're buying and selling frequently, you must consider brokerage fees.

Let's assume a trading commission of $7 per transaction. If you buy and sell the IVV ETF 10 times in a year, that adds an additional $140 in trading costs, effectively raising your total expense to 0.11% for the year.

Moreover, beyond trading commissions, some ETFs impose additional fees or hidden costs that can significantly impact overall profitability. For example, certain ETFs charge transaction fees which can be as high as $20 for each trade if held less than a specified timeframe.

If you were to purchase the SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY), which has an expense ratio of 0.09% but also incurs $20 in transaction fees if sold within 30 days, that can swiftly change the cost dynamics.

If you bought SPY for $400, and sold it at around the same price within a month, that one-time transaction cost alone translates to a 5% fee on your investment.

A 30-day screening process for evaluating ETF costs could look like this: 1. List all ETFs you’re interested in: Build a shortlist based on your investment goals and criteria. 2. Break down their expense ratios: Compare these ratios since, while seemingly insignificant, over time they can compound. 3. Account for average trading commissions: Track how often you plan to trade; ascertain the cost for each transaction as demonstrated. 4. Factor in potential taxes on gains: Capital gains tax can further deduct from your returns.

If you sell an ETF for a profit, a capital gains tax rate of around 15% will apply, affecting your net returns.

Consider a scenario: you invest $5,000 into the Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI), which boasts an expense ratio of 0.04%. If you buy and hold the ETF for ten years, you might expect minimal charges.

However, if you also make four trades a year with $5 commission fees, this adds another $20 annually. While you’re paying only a slight extra fee that may not seem significant at the outset, over the period of ten years, that $200 could have otherwise been reinvested, impacting your compounded growth, especially in a bull market.

This thorough analysis helps uncover the total cost of ownership, ensuring you don’t fall victim to hidden pitfalls that could diminish your investment returns over time.

Real Scenario: The Cost of Ignoring Diversification

### Real Scenario: The Cost of Ignoring Diversification

A glaring mistake often made by new ETF investors is neglecting diversification within their portfolios. Consider a novice investor, Alex, who invests $10,000 entirely in the Direxion Daily Financial Bull 3X Shares (FAS), betting on a strong performance of financial stocks in the short term.

Initially, his gamble pays off; within six months, FAS rises by 30%, increasing his investment to approximately $13,000. However, this short-term success leads to overconfidence and a sense of security that misguides Alex’s future decisions.

After six months, headlines begin to surface about rising interest rates, prompting fears of a recession that could affect financial institutions adversely. As market conditions shift, FAS plummets by 50% over the next quarter due to underperformance in the banking sector, leaving Alex with just $6,500—a stark reminder that heavy concentrations in a single industry can be perilous.

In contrast, another new investor, Jamie, adopts a diversified strategy. Instead of placing all bets on one sector, Jamie allocates the same $10,000 across four ETFs: $4,000 in VOO (S&P 500), $2,500 in the iShares Core U.S.

Aggregate Bond ETF (AGG), $2,500 in a popular international fund like the iShares MSCI Emerging Markets ETF (EEM), and $1,000 in a sector-specific ETF focused on technology, such as the Technology Select Sector SPDR Fund (XLT).

Over the same six-month period, while the S&P 500 mirrors the initial growth of FAS with a 30% increase, Jamie's overall investment strategy holds steady through volatility, benefiting from the stability of the bond market, which has experienced a modest 4% gain even in uncertain economic climates.

Eventually, when the downturn hits and the financial sector slumps, Jamie finds that her diversified portfolio shrinks only by about 10%, resulting in a total value of around $8,800—a far less dramatic decline than Alex’s.

This strategic allocation not only mitigates risk but also provides Alex a roadmap for what could have been, highlighting the value of diversification. By reviewing investments every quarter, Jamie maintains her portfolio in line with shifting market conditions, adjusting allocations to ensure she stays aligned with her risk tolerance and financial objectives.

This practice becomes invaluable, especially during unpredictable market cycles. The scenario of relying heavily on a single ETF underscores why diversification is not just a strategy, but a crucial safeguard against unforeseen market events.

Timing Matters: The Risks of Market Timing with ETFs

Timing Matters: The Risks of Market Timing with ETFs

Attempting to time the market remains a costly mistake for many new ETF investors. Many believe they can sell their holdings just before a downturn and then repurchase them at lower prices, but the reality is far more complex.

For instance, studies conducted by Dalbar have shown that missing just the 10 best trading days in a 20-year period can lead to a nearly 50% reduction in overall returns compared to a buy-and-hold strategy.

Investors who remained fully invested in the market over a decade achieved an average annual return of about 7%. In contrast, those who attempted to time their entries and exits often underperformed, with some losing as much as 2% annually.

Consider two hypothetical investors: Alex and Jamie. Alex decides to invest a set amount of $500 each month into a diversified ETF that tracks the S&P 500. Over 10 years, despite some market volatility, Alex benefits from dollar-cost averaging, enabling him to buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high. By staying consistent with his investments, he amasses a significant nest egg.

On the flip side, Jamie believes she can outsmart the market. In 2018, she sees a dip in the market and decides to sell her holdings, thinking she can buy back in at a lower price. However, the market rebounds quickly, and she misses out on the substantial gains made in the subsequent months.

For example, if the S&P 500 increased by 20% in the following year post-dip, Jamie’s decision to sell could cost her around $6,000 on a $30,000 initial investment.

Furthermore, new investors often succumb to market hype, making impulsive decisions driven by fear or greed. For example, during the COVID-19 market crash in early 2020, many investors panicked and sold at a loss, missing the rapid recovery that followed.

Data showed that individuals who remained invested during this volatility eventually saw their portfolios not only recover but also expand significantly due to the swift market rebound.

A sensible strategy is to adopt dollar-cost averaging: investing a fixed amount regularly over time. By committing to a monthly investment, such as $500 in a diversified ETF, new investors can mitigate risks associated with market timing.

Setting clear investment goals—like planning for retirement, funding education, or saving for a home—can help maintain focus amidst market fluctuations. Remember, the longer you stay invested, the more potential rewards you can reap, reinforcing the value of a disciplined and methodical approach to investing in ETFs.

Next Steps: Building a Resilient ETF Strategy

Creating a robust ETF investment strategy is crucial for avoiding common pitfalls and securing your financial future. A practical step to start is to establish an investment checklist that includes: 1.

Assessing your risk tolerance, 2. Evaluating potential ETFs based on goals, 3. Monthly monitoring of your portfolio performance, 4. Highlighting possible rebalancing intervals. Schedule to review your portfolio at least every six months.

This disciplined approach helps ensure that your investments align with your financial goals and market conditions. When evaluating ETFs, stick to proven options like VOO or AGG, and take time to research sectors before diving in.

Avoid jumping on the latest fad fund simply because it’s trending. In essence, commit to an ongoing education of the ETF landscape, and don't hesitate to consult financial advisors or reliable online resources.

Taking these steps will pave the way for better decision-making and ultimately, improved investment outcomes.

FAQ

What should I look for when choosing an ETF?
Consider the expense ratio, historical performance, underlying assets, and how well it aligns with your investment strategy.

How can I diversify my ETF portfolio effectively?
Invest across various asset classes and sectors, incorporating a mix of domestic and international funds.

Can I lose money investing in ETFs?
Yes, while ETFs provide diversification, they are still subject to market risks.
