How Beginners Can Start Making Money with Online Courses

Explore a straightforward guide for beginners to create and sell online courses, overcoming common challenges in monetizing their knowledge.

Make Money • Updated: 2026-03-15 06:01:23

TL;DR

Begin your online course journey in just 30 days! Validate your course idea, create engaging content, and leverage effective marketing to reach potential students. Avoid common mistakes, such as neglecting audience engagement, to maximize your earnings potential.

Why Beginners Should Learn to Make Money with Online Courses

Many new creators jump into online courses believing that sharing their knowledge will guarantee income. They may assume that merely recording videos or writing content is enough to attract students and generate revenue.

However, beginners often face high failure rates due to a lack of understanding of the market and audience needs. For instance, beginners may create a course on a topic they're passionate about but fail to validate this choice with potential students.

Why the Default Approach Fails for Course Creators

Most first-time course creators fall into the trap of believing that high-quality content is the magic key to success. They assume that once their course is created, students will flock to it, neglecting critical components like effective marketing, brand building, audience targeting, and the right platform selection.

For instance, think about a beginner who launches a photography course on platforms like Udemy or Teachable. Without a solid marketing strategy, they might see only 50 enrollments in the first month, despite pouring countless hours into producing stunning video content.

In contrast, a well-structured marketing plan could lead to 200 or more enrollments simply through targeted social media advertising and leveraging an email list of potential students.

Moreover, aligning course content with the audience’s specific pain points is paramount. A course on "advanced photo editing techniques" might flop if the intended audience consists mainly of beginners who need foundational skills first.

If the creator ignores this mismatch, they risk not only poor sales but also negative reviews, which can taint their reputation for future courses.

This misconception often leads novice creators to misallocate their time and resources. Instead of investing in marketing efforts or audience engagement, they might spend 80% of their time perfecting content that won’t resonate.

For example, if a course creator dedicates three months developing their material but only allocates one week to promoting it, they may miss the opportunity to connect with potential customers and drive insights into what they truly want from the class.

Consider a case where a course creator dedicates two months to audience analysis—conducting surveys, engaging on social media, and hosting free webinars to gauge interest.

They might discover that prospective students are eager for a course on "basic photo composition" instead of advanced techniques. By pivoting to address this clear demand, they could expect their course to attract four times the initial target enrollments in the first month, translating to a more sustainable income stream.

The Core Workflow: Creating Your First Online Course in 30 Days

To create your first online course in just 30 days, follow this structured approach: 1. Week 1: Research & Validation - Identify your niche and validate your course idea through surveys or social media polls. - Objective: Ensure there's a demand. 2. Week 2: Content Development - Start drafting content, segments, and outlines. Use tools like Google Docs for collaborative input. - Objective: Gather feedback from peers or potential students. 3. Week 3: Production - Record video lessons or create slides depending on your course method. Tools like Loom or Canva can be helpful. - Objective: Aim for completion of all course materials. 4. Week 4: Marketing & Launching - Set up your landing page and ensure optimizations like SEO. Use platforms like MailChimp for email campaigns. - Objective: Drive traffic and convert leads into students during launch.

A Real Example: How Lisa Made $2,000 in Her First Month

Consider Lisa, a complete beginner who decided to turn her passion for photography into an online course. Over the span of 30 days, she dedicated around 15 hours each week to meticulously following a structured plan.

First, she conducted surveys on social media to gauge interest and pinpoint what beginner photographers really wanted to learn—common issues like composition, lighting, and post-processing techniques.

With this valuable feedback, she developed a comprehensive course outline covering essential topics.

To create engaging content, Lisa invested in a quality camera and screen recording software, spending approximately $400 on tools and resources.

After recording her lessons and editing them, she spent time designing eye-catching thumbnails and promotional posts for her social media channels, mainly Instagram and Pinterest.

She allocated about 10 hours to create visually appealing marketing materials that would attract her target audience.

On launch day, she priced her course at $20, aiming for affordability while still covering her initial costs. Lisa shared her course with her followers and joined photography groups online, ensuring she reached beginner photographers eager to improve their skills.

Her marketing tactics, including a limited-time promotional discount, helped her reach 100 enrollments within the first month, resulting in $2,000 in revenue.

Alongside this success, Lisa learned the importance of setting realistic expectations. She faced challenges like occasional technical difficulties and the initial slow growth of her social media presence, requiring persistence and adaptation.

In one scenario, she realized that a video addressing common photography mistakes resonated well, prompting her to create additional free content to drive traffic to her course.

Mistakes That Cause Beginners to Struggle in Course Creation

Beginners frequently make several crucial mistakes that can stifle their online course success. One common misstep is neglecting marketing, believing that the platform will do the work for them.

This overlooks the importance of building an audience and promotional strategies. Additionally, some creators rush their course development—skipping the critical step of feedback collection from potential students.

Finally, a lack of follow-up on student engagement can lead to decreased course completion rates and poor reviews. The tradeoff here is that while developing high-quality content requires time, cutting corners can jeopardize long-term success.

What to Use Next: Scaling Your Online Course Portfolio

Once you've successfully launched your first course, consider expanding your portfolio for increased passive income. Explore additional topics that complement your initial course or delve deeper into your niche.

For example, if Lisa creates a photography course, she may add courses on photo editing or thriving on social media. Another viable strategy is to bundle courses together and offer discounts for package deals.

Planning a course roadmap can help you forecast income potential over months or years, allowing for sustainable growth

FAQ

What skills do I need to create an online course?
Essential skills include content creation, basic video editing, and marketing capabilities to promote your course.

How much can I realistically earn from online courses?
Earnings vary widely, but successful courses can generate hundreds to thousands of dollars monthly, depending on audience size and pricing.

How do I choose the right platform for selling my course?
Evaluate platforms based on cost, features, and audience reach; popular options include Teachable, Kajabi, and Udemy.
