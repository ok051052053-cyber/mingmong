6 Mistakes Novice Investors Make When Choosing ETFs

Avoid costly errors in your ETF investments by understanding common mistakes novice investors make when selecting funds.

Investing • Updated: 2026-03-14 16:19:20

TL;DR

Avoid common ETF mistakes by focusing on expenses and performance metrics. Start with low-cost ETFs like SPY or VTI. Implement a routine screening process to guide your choices and review them regularly.

Quick Answer

Quick Answer

For beginners looking to invest in ETFs, understanding your objectives is crucial. Start with broad market ETFs like the SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) or the Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI), using an allocation of $1,000.

A common mistake is overlooking fees; for example, while SPY has an expense ratio of 0.09%, some lesser-known funds may exceed 1%, significantly eroding your returns over time.

If you invested $5,000 and the market returns 7% annually, a 1% fee could cost you around $50 each year.

Another pitfall is choosing funds based solely on past performance. A fund that returned 20% last year might not perform similarly in the future, and the risk of "chasing returns" can lead to poor long-term strategies.

Instead, prioritize funds with low expense ratios, transparency, and consistent tracking of their indices.

For instance, a novice investor may consider investing in a trendy tech-focused ETF that showed a 35% return last year. However, if they neglect to research its underlying assets or expense ratio—which can be 0.75%—they may overlook a more stable option like the Vanguard Information Technology ETF (VGT), which has a 0.10% ratio and a proven track record.

Why Most Beginners Pick the Wrong ETFs

### Why Most Beginners Pick the Wrong ETFs

Many novice investors fall victim to the allure of flashy performance numbers instead of analyzing the fundamentals. A common mistake is equating past performance with future success.

For example, if an ETF returned an impressive 25% last year, new investors might assume it will yield similar returns in the subsequent year.

However, historically, many high-performing funds can see drastic fluctuations; the S&P 500, for instance, saw an average return of about 10% over the long term, but individual years can vary widely.

Beyond performance, many overlook ETF fees, which accumulate over time. A seemingly small management fee of 0.5% might not seem significant initially, but over a 30-year investment horizon with a $10,000 initial investment, that small fee could compound to cost you over $17,000 in lost gains!

Consider Sarah, a novice investor who excitedly purchased an emerging market ETF that boasted a 30% return in the previous year. Intrigued by this number, she invested $5,000 without researching the underlying fundamentals or fee structure.

Unbeknownst to her, the ETF had a management fee of 1.2%, significantly higher than an average market ETF. By the time she realizes the potential of her other investments underperformed due to high costs, Sarah has missed out on opportunities that would have been less costly and potentially more stable.

Investors also tend to focus only on one or two metrics, such as expense ratios or past annual returns. However, factors like liquidity, tax efficiency, and the ETF’s underlying index composition are just as critical.

A 30-Day Screening Process

To avoid the pitfalls of ETF selection, implement a structured screening process over 30 days. Follow these steps: 1. Define Your Goals - Determine your investment strategy, whether you're looking for growth, income, or diversification. 2. Research Funds - Identify ETFs that match your goals. Look into funds that track indices you believe in. 3. Analyze Fees - Focus on ETFs with low expense ratios and transparent costs. 4. Evaluate Performance - Check their tracking error and historical performance relative to the underlying index. 5. Subscribe to Alerts - Set alerts or follow updates on your chosen ETFs to stay informed. 6. Review Periodically - After 30 days, reassess the ETFs based on real performance data

A Real Scenario for Beginner Investors

### A Real Scenario for Beginner Investors

Imagine a beginner investor named Sarah, who decides to start her investment journey with just $100 each month. Rather than waiting until she can accumulate a larger sum, Sarah embraces a recurring investment plan to dip her toes into the world of ETFs.

Each month, she allocates $60 into a broad market ETF, such as the Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI), which offers diversified exposure to the entire U.S.

stock market.

With her $20 allocation towards a dividend ETF, Sarah opts for the Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF (SCHD), which provides a steady income stream and potential for capital appreciation.

The remaining $20 is set aside in cash or put into a low-risk bond fund, like the iShares U.S. Treasury Bond ETF (GOVT), offering stability and reducing volatility in her portfolio.

In the first few months, Sarah's portfolio might appear modest, with her total contributions amounting to just $300 by the end of three months.

However, the real benefit lies in her commitment to consistency. By adopting this disciplined approach, she not only becomes comfortable with the inevitable market fluctuations but also learns the value of dollar-cost averaging, which helps mitigate the risks associated with market timing.

For instance, if the market takes a downturn in month four and her broad market ETF dips by 10%, Sarah won't panic. Instead, she'll take advantage of the lower prices, investing her $100 as planned.

This approach, over time, allows her to purchase more shares when prices are down, ultimately enhancing her long-term returns.

By the end of her first year, Sarah has contributed $1,200. The market has experienced periods of volatility, but she has remained steadfast.

Mistakes That Cost Real Money

In the world of ETF investments, two frequent missteps can be particularly detrimental: ignoring diversification and failing to rebalance your portfolio.

For instance, a novice might invest heavily in a single sector during a rally, leading to an unbalanced high-risk portfolio. If tech stocks tumble, the entire portfolio can suffer significantly.

An example would be if an investor poured 70% of their capital into a tech ETF; a downturn could mean losing nearly half their investment within months.

Moreover, neglecting to rebalance can lead to increased risk over time, as winners take up a larger portion of the portfolio. Investors should aim for a diversified mix that includes different sectors and geographies to mitigate risks.

Which Tools or ETFs Make This Simpler

For beginners, leveraging the right tools can simplify ETF selection and monitoring. Start with platforms like Morningstar or ETF.com to analyze various ETF metrics, including fees and historical returns.

Choose ETFs like the iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) for a broad market exposure with a low expense ratio of 0.03%. For automated investment management, consider robo-advisors like Betterment that offer tailored ETF portfolios.

When first starting, avoid overly complex ETFs or niche funds that require a high level of expertise. Stick to well-established funds and gradually expand your knowledge.

FAQ

What are some top ETFs for beginners?
Popular choices include the Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI) and the iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) due to their broad market coverage and low fees.

How can I minimize risks when investing in ETFs?
Diversifying your portfolio across different sectors and asset classes while regularly rebalancing can help mitigate risks significantly.

What should I look for in an ETF?
Focus on low expense ratios, clear investment objectives, and reliable historical performance before making your selection.

How frequently should I review my ETF holdings?
Aim to review your ETF portfolio at least quarterly to ensure alignment with your investment goals.
