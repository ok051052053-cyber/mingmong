Best AI Stocks for Long-Term Investors: Practical Picks and Real Steps (2023)

Realistic advice for US and EU investors starting with AI stocks. See which AI stocks and ETFs fit long-term strategies, common mistakes to avoid, and how to work through portfolio set-up.

Investing • Updated: 2026-03-10 14:26:44

TL;DR

Start small with an AI-focused ETF like IRBO or BOTZ. Mix in a modest amount of NVIDIA or Alphabet for extra growth. Stick to a repeatable monthly investing process and avoid single-stock hype. Watch out for high fees and panic-selling during market swings. Expect long-term gains, not overnight wins—steady compounding beats risky bets.

Quick Answer: Where Beginners Should Start with AI Stocks

Start with a small, diversified position using a broad AI-focused ETF like Global X Robotics & Artificial Intelligence ETF (BOTZ) or iShares Robotics and Artificial Intelligence Multisector ETF (IRBO).

For most new investors, $250 to $1,000 is a realistic starting range—enough to get exposure without overcommitting. For pure stock picks, consider allocating a small percentage (20% or less) into large-cap leaders like NVIDIA (NVDA) or Alphabet (GOOGL), while keeping the bulk in the ETF to reduce risk.

Beginners should avoid going all-in on any single high-flyer or highly volatile small-cap stock at the start. Tradeoff: ETF diversification means giving up the chance of outsized gains from one big winner, but it is the more practical decision for most.

Why Most Beginners Pick the Wrong AI Stocks

A common mistake: chasing the most talked-about AI companies, expecting overnight results. Plenty of beginners see NVIDIA’s 2023 growth and assume every AI stock is a ticket to outsized returns.

In practice, many hyped companies experience dramatic swings or lack sustainable business models. Another trap is underestimating the volatility. AI is a growth story, but timing is unpredictable—even proven leaders can drop 25% or more in a quarter.

Tradeoff: Single-stock bets offer high upside but also expose beginners to painful, sudden drawdowns. The decision to buy hot IPOs like C3.ai (AI) or Palantir (PLTR) early, for example, has led to sharp losses for many who entered on buzz alone.

Beginners often ignore sector concentration risk: too much tech, too little diversification. A more measured approach means expecting some years of mediocre or negative returns, even from 'winners.'

Key mistakes: - Believing that 'AI exposure' equals automatic profit - Allocating too much to unproven small-caps - Ignoring fees, taxes, and switching costs involved in active trading

A 30-Day Screening Process for Picking AI Stocks

Commit to a repeatable 30-day routine before deploying cash. This monthly rhythm helps avoid impulsive buys based on hype cycles. Here’s a reliable 4-step process for evaluating AI stocks and ETFs: 1. List: Create your initial watchlist of AI-focused ETFs (e.g., BOTZ, IRBO, ROBO) and top stocks (e.g., NVIDIA, Alphabet, ASML, Microsoft). 2. Screen: Each week, review earnings reports and recent news for major changes in guidance or sector trends. 3. Compare: Use free research tools (Yahoo Finance, Seeking Alpha) to benchmark P/E ratios, growth rates, and volatility. 4. Allocate: Check your allocation and re-balance if one stock/ETF exceeds 30% of your AI segment.

In practice, beginners who follow a 30-day cadence are less likely to overpay for headline momentum. Scenario: You notice that Microsoft’s quarterly results highlight real AI revenue growth, while a smaller pick like UiPath still struggles for profitability.

The numbers clarify which story is showing up in actual results. Repeating these steps monthly gives you extra time for reflection—a vital tradeoff that often prevents rushed decisions.

A Simple Beginner Example With Numbers

Let’s say you start with $1,000 earmarked for long term AI exposure. A realistic allocation for a US or EU beginner might look like:

  • $700 into IRBO (broad AI ETF, low barrier, 0.47% fee)
  • $200 into NVIDIA (high-growth, high-volatility leader)
  • $100 into Alphabet (AI-driven profit engine with diversification)

Each month, you add $100—repeating the same split. In one year, you’ve invested $2,200 total, with roughly $1,540 in IRBO, $440 in NVIDIA, and $220 in Alphabet (excluding growth).

In practice, fees eat about $10 annually on the ETF portion. Tradeoff: ETFs smooth out some volatility, but you’ll see smaller gains during massive breakout rallies. During a tech correction (scenario: 2022), the ETF could drop 20%, while NVIDIA’s share may sink 35–40% in weeks. The key decision is stick with the plan or pull back. Most beginners panic-sell at the worst moment, locking in losses—a classic mistake.

Mistakes That Cost Real Money: What to Watch and Avoid

Two beginner mistakes can derail a long term AI investing plan quickly. First: All-in bets on one company, only to see a single bad quarter erase a year’s gains. Example: someone puts $1,000 into Palantir at $25/share in early 2021—six months later, it’s down to $10.

Second: Ignoring the drag from fees, taxes, and trading commissions. With an AI ETF, a 0.68% annual fee (like what you’ll see with some niche funds) eats away at compounding. Over 10 years, that’s hundreds lost vs.

lower-fee broad market ETFs.

A subtle decision: when to cut losses versus double down. Emotional reactivity often leads to another mistake—swapping a solid ETF for a trending stock during a hot news cycle. Remember, long term winners are not always the flashiest.

Scenario: If you panic and sell everything at a 20% drawdown, you realize losses; if you stick to a small, regular-investment schedule, you ride out volatility. Tradeoff: higher short-term losses versus missing multi-year compounding. Most beginners regret abandoning the plan halfway.

Which Tools or ETFs Make This Simpler—and Which to Ignore

Best for new investors: IRBO and BOTZ ETFs—both offer instant AI diversification, relatively low fees, and monthly liquidity. NVIDIA is the core single-stock growth play for those willing to handle more volatility.

Alphabet works as a defensive AI bet with a long track record. Don’t bother with overly complex platforms or niche AI ETFs with high fees (>0.7%) unless you have a specific, well-researched conviction and a large portfolio.

Setup difficulty: Very low—major brokerage platforms like Vanguard, Charles Schwab (US), or DEGIRO (EU) allow easy purchases of both ETFs and core AI stocks.

Main strength: Diversification, ease of maintenance, and the ability to set up monthly auto-investments.

Main weakness: You’ll never beat the single-stock lottery winners and you may feel underwhelmed during big upswings. Tradeoff: smoother growth, fewer headaches.

My verdict: For most long term beginners, stick to 2–3 major products, automate contributions, and keep researching sector leaders quarterly. Avoid complex strategies until your portfolio exceeds $10,000 or you have six months of repeated investing experience.

FAQ

What are the top AI stocks to invest in for long-term growth?
NVIDIA (NVDA), Alphabet (GOOGL), and Microsoft (MSFT) are among the most reliable large-cap AI stocks for long-term growth. ETFs like IRBO and BOTZ provide diversified exposure for most beginners. Risk is lower with ETFs, but single stocks can offer higher rewards if you can handle volatility.

How can I evaluate the risks of investing in AI stocks?
Check for high price/earnings ratios, recent profitability trends, historical volatility, and sector concentration. Consider your own risk tolerance and how much loss you can absorb in any one investment before proceeding. Diversification and sizing positions conservatively are key to reducing risk.

What’s a realistic monthly amount to start investing in AI stocks?
Many beginners start with $100–$250 per month, split between an AI ETF and one or two high-conviction stocks. Consistency and a long time horizon matter more than timing your buy in perfectly. Automatic investments can help remove emotion from the process.

Should I invest in AI stocks directly, or use an ETF?
Direct stocks offer higher potential returns but also bring more volatility and the risk of single-stock collapse. Broad AI ETFs are best for risk-managed, hands-off long term growth and are smarter for most new investors. Try mixing both with more weight on ETFs to start.

What is the biggest beginner mistake with AI investing?
Going all-in on one hyped stock, ignoring diversification, and panicking during inevitable drawdowns. These mistakes can turn temporary paper losses into permanent ones. Stick to a plan, diversify, and avoid emotional trades.
